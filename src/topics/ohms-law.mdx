# Ohm's law

**V = IR.** Three quantities, one constraint. Fix any two; the third is forced.

<OhmsExplorer />

The dots are current — their speed shows how much charge flows per second. The graph plots V against I: a straight line from the origin with slope 1/R. Slide voltage and the operating point moves along the line. Slide resistance and the line pivots — steeper means lower resistance, more current per volt.

That straight line *is* the definition of an ohmic material. A component whose V–I curve bends (a diode, a thermistor) is non-ohmic: V = IR still holds at each instant, but R shifts with conditions.

## Power

P = IV. Substitute V = IR: **P = I²R**. Substitute I = V/R: **P = V²/R**.

The square is where circuits bite. Double current through a fixed resistor and power quadruples. This is why short circuits destroy things: halving R at fixed V doubles I but quadruples P.

## Scope

**Ohmic:** metals at stable temperature, carbon resistors within rated power, most passive components in their linear range.

**Non-ohmic:** diodes, LEDs, thermistors, filament bulbs. R changes with operating conditions.

**AC:** generalizes to V = IZ where Z is complex impedance. Same constraint shape, complex arithmetic.

**Kirchhoff's laws** compose multiple Ohm's law constraints into a system — KVL (voltages around loops sum to zero) and KCL (currents at nodes sum to zero).
